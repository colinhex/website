Why Computer Science?

I have always been interested in technology, and it did not take long for me to take up programming and mathematics as favorites in my studies. An entry point towards computer science for me was the realization that no matter the sub-subject or issue, time and sustained engagement can make a fool be a rookie and let a rookie become savvy. I discovered some affinity for structure and some sense of beauty in code. Be it in the predictability and elegance of algorithms or in the pragmatic and creative way of heuristics.

There are right ways to do things, by design or metrics and if you stumble across it by research or are told once you are sure not to make the same mistake twice, as it probably gave you more head-aches than you like to admit. If you are thorough enough and have a curious outlook that welcomes criticism you very likely prevent most mistakes from the get-go. Being empathetic towards others, this makes results especially satisfying: knowing you worked on something that is persistent, secure and wonâ€™t cause head-aches.

Through the course of my degree I was dabbling first in Java, then MatLab and Python, then some C++ and SQL and, at the time of writing, in the basics of web-development. My biggest "Ahaa"-moment was not some deeply-buried-in-the-stack exception, though there were quite a few of those, but the sheer size of all individual and interconnected software-systems that span the globe and drive the economy, society and politics of today and tomorrow.

Learning about Networks and Security

I had a lot of fun writing the back-end for a 'Secret Sharing' module in the scope of the Basel Citizen Net repository some time ago. The project and the corresponding lectures were a great introduction to networks, security and encryption. An unprompted addition from me was using the shamir algorithm which takes secrets of a maximum of 16 bytes and reordering & repackaging the data until it would support secrets of 4 Kb, keeping linear complexity.
